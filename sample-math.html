On the beauty of convergence

There is something quietly profound about a sequence that approaches a value it never actually reaches. Consider $a_n = 1 - \frac{1}{n}$. As $n$ grows, the terms get closer and closer to $1$ — but no individual term is $1$. The limit exists; the arrival never comes.

Formally, $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists an $N$ such that for all $n > N$: $$|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$$

This is the epsilon-N definition. It's a statement about control: however tightly you draw the circle around $L$, the sequence eventually stays inside it.

What I find beautiful here is not the formalism — it's the philosophy embedded in it. The definition doesn't say the sequence reaches $L$. It says the sequence can be made arbitrarily close. Closeness, not arrival. Approach, not destination.

The Cauchy criterion

A sequence is Cauchy if its terms eventually become close to each other — regardless of whether you know the limit in advance: $$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists N:\ \forall m, n > N,\ |a_m - a_n| < \varepsilon$$

In complete metric spaces, Cauchy sequences always converge. The self-consistency of the sequence — its internal coherence — is enough to guarantee a limit. You don't need to know where you're going. You just need to ensure you're not drifting apart from yourself.

I find that surprisingly moving.

Asymptotes as a way of being

An asymptote is a line a curve approaches but never reaches. In calculus, it signals a kind of controlled divergence — the function grows, but in a bounded direction.

I've started thinking of certain personal qualities this way. Patience. Precision. Equanimity. You approach them; you don't arrive. The approaching is the practice. The limit is real even if you never touch it.

There's a word in Urdu — sabr — usually translated as patience, but it carries more: endurance, steadiness, trust in the process. Mathematically, I think of it as the Cauchy condition applied to character. Stay close to yourself. Don't drift. The convergence will follow.
